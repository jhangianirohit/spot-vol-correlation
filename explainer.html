Spot-Vol Correlation

A framework for understanding, decomposing, and trading FX risk reversals through the lens of spot-vol beta.

1. The Risk Reversal — What Are We Pricing?

The 25-delta risk reversal (RR) is a structure that buys a 25d call and sells a 25d put on the same notional and expiry. It is quoted as the vol differential: RR = σ(25d call) − σ(25d put).

A negative RR (e.g., −1.0%) means puts trade at higher vol than calls — the market expects vol to rise when spot falls. But what exactly determines the magnitude of the RR? That's the question this framework answers.

Core insight: The RR is a vanna instrument. Its value comes from the expected correlation between spot and vol — the "spot-vol beta."

2. Vanna — The Greek That Drives the RR

Vanna measures how an option's vega changes when spot moves (equivalently, how delta changes when vol moves).

Important notation

Two functions that look similar but are completely different:

N(x) = cumulative normal (CDF) — this gives delta. N(−0.6745) = 0.25
n(x) = normal density (PDF) — the bell curve height. n(−0.6745) = 0.3178

The vanna formula uses the density n (small n), not the CDF N (big N).

The vanna formula

Vanna = ∂Vega / ∂Spot = −n(d₁) × d₂ / σ

For a 25-delta call, vanna is positive: when spot rises, the call moves toward ATM and its vega increases. For a 25-delta put, vanna is negative. The RR (long call, short put) therefore has large positive net vanna.

Worked example — EURUSD 3M 25d Call, 100 mio EUR

Setup Spot = 1.1415, ATM vol = 7.25%, T = 91 days, Notional = 100 mio EUR
25d call strike = 1.1705 (+290 pips from spot)

Step 1: Find d₁ and d₂

For a 25-delta call, d₁ is fixed by the delta condition:

Δ = N(d₁) = 0.25 → d₁ = N⁻¹(0.25) = −0.6745

d₂ = d₁ − σ√T = −0.6745 − 0.0725 × √(91/365) = −0.6745 − 0.0362 = −0.7107

Step 2: Compute vega

Vega = S × n(d₁) × √T = 1.1415 × 0.3178 × √(91/365) = 0.1811 per unit σ, per unit notional

For 100 mio, per 1 vol point (1 vol pt = 0.01 in sigma):

Vega = 0.1811 × 0.01 × 100,000,000 = $181,122 per vol point

Meaning: if vol moves from 7.25% to 8.25% (+1 vol pt), the option gains $181,122.

Step 3: Compute vanna

Vanna = −n(d₁) × d₂ / σ = −0.3178 × (−0.7107) / 0.0725 = +3.115 per unit S, per unit σ

To convert to "per 1% spot move, per 1 vol point, for 100 mio":

Vanna$ = 3.115 × 0.01 × S × 0.01 × Notional
       = 3.115 × 0.01 × 1.1415 × 0.01 × 100,000,000
       = $35,558 per vol pt per 1% spot
In plain English: If EURUSD rises 1% (from 1.1415 to 1.1529), the option's vega increases by $35,558. It goes from $181,122 to $216,680 per vol point. Why? The call was 25-delta (290 pips OTM). After spot rises 1% (114 pips), it's closer to ATM, so it has more vol exposure.

Why the vanna of the 25d RR simplifies

For the RR (call − put), the individual vannas are:

Vanna(call) = −n(d₁) × d₂_call / σ = −n(d₁) × (d₁_call − σ√T) / σ
Vanna(put)  = −n(d₁) × d₂_put  / σ = −n(d₁) × (d₁_put  − σ√T) / σ

Subtracting:

Vanna_RR = −n(d₁)/σ × [(d₁_call − σ√T) − (d₁_put − σ√T)]
         = −n(d₁)/σ × [d₁_call − d₁_put]
                                ↑ the σ√T cancels!

         = −n(d₁)/σ × [(−0.6745) − (+0.6745)]
         = −n(d₁)/σ × (−1.3490)
         = 2 × |d₁| × n(d₁) / σ
         = 2 × 0.6745 × 0.3178 / σ
         = 0.4288 / σ

The σ√T terms from d₂ cancel in the subtraction. The dollar vanna of the RR is nearly constant across tenors (it varies only slightly through d₂). What changes dramatically is vega (which scales with √T), so the vanna-to-vega ratio — which is what determines the RR — depends on tenor. The formula accounts for this through the √T term.

3. Beta — The Spot-Vol Sensitivity

We define beta as:

β = vol change (in vol points) per 1% spot move

For example, β = −0.85 for EURUSD 1M means: if EURUSD rises 1%, ATM vol falls by 0.85 vol points (e.g., 7.70% → 6.85%).

Negative beta is the norm for most pairs: spot down = risk-off = vol up.

4. From Beta to Fair RR — The Vanna P&L Argument

If we hold the RR and spot moves, we earn vanna P&L:

Vanna P&L per step = Vanna_RR × ΔSpot × ΔVol

If vol moves with spot (beta ≠ 0), then ΔVol = β × ΔSpot, and:

Vanna P&L = Vanna_RR × β × (ΔSpot)²

Over the option's life, the expected total:

E[Σ Vanna P&L] = Vanna_RR × β × E[Σ(ΔSpot)²] = Vanna_RR × β × S² × σ² × T

! The Scaling Factor

The formula above assumes vanna stays constant. But after spot moves, the strikes are no longer at 25-delta — vanna changes. We tested this with Monte Carlo (200k paths) and found that the expected vanna over the option's life ≈ 50% of inception vanna. This ~0.5 scaling factor is remarkably stable across tenors and vol levels.

E[Vanna P&L] ≈ 0.5 × Vanna_RR × β × S² × σ² × T

Converting P&L to Vol Premium

The fair RR is the vol premium where the cost of paying the skew equals the expected vanna P&L:

avg_vega × RR = E[Vanna P&L]
RR = 0.5 × (Vanna_RR / avg_vega) × β × S² × σ² × T

Now substitute Vanna_RR = 0.4288/σ and avg_vega = S × 0.3178 × √T:

Vanna_RR / avg_vega = 0.4288 / (σ × S × 0.3178 × √T) = 1.349 / (S × σ × √T)

After cancellation, everything collapses to:

β ≈ 1.48 × RR / (ATM × √T)

Where β is in vol points per 1% spot, RR and ATM are both in %, and T is in years. The constant 1.48 = 1/(2 × 0.5 × 0.6745).

Worked Example: EURUSD 1M ATM vol = 7.70%, RR = −1.26%, T = 30/365

β = 1.48 × (−1.26) / (7.70 × √(30/365))
  = 1.48 × (−1.26) / (7.70 × 0.287)
  = 1.48 × (−1.26) / 2.208
  = −0.85

Meaning: EURUSD rises 1% → vol falls 0.85 vol pts (7.70% → 6.85%).

5. The Market Maker Proof

We verified the formula with a Monte Carlo simulation:

1 Price the RR at the model skew (call at ATM + RR/2, put at ATM − RR/2)

2 Simulate 200k spot paths where vol moves by β × spot return each day

3 Delta hedge daily, mark to market at each step

4 Measure: does the vanna P&L earned through hedging equal the skew premium paid?

Result: Net P&L / Skew premium ≈ −0.03 across all betas (−3 to +3). The market maker breaks even to within 3%. The RR price correctly reflects the beta.

6. Cross-Sectional Comparability — RR/ATM

From the formula β = 1.48 × RR / (ATM × √T), two pairs with the same RR/ATM ratio at the same tenor have identical betas, regardless of vol level.

This works because vanna scales as 1/σ — higher vol pairs have lower vanna per unit notional, which exactly offsets the higher vol's contribution to quadratic variation. So RR/ATM is the sufficient statistic for cross-pair comparison.

PairATMRRRR/ATMBeta
EURUSD7.70%−1.26%−0.164−0.85
AUDUSD10.50%−1.72%−0.164−0.85

Same RR/ATM → same beta. The vol level doesn't matter.

7. Cross-Pair Decomposition — The USD Approach

Can we predict a cross pair's RR from the USD pair RRs? For example, EURCNH from EURUSD and USDCNH?

The approach: EURCNH = EURUSD × USDCNH. When EURCNH moves 1%:

1 How much does each USD pair move? (spot decomposition via regression)

2 How does each USD pair's vol respond? (the USD pair betas)

3 How do the vol changes combine into EURCNH vol? (vol sensitivities ∂σ_cross/∂σ_leg)

β(cross) = vol_w₁ × β₁ × spot_w₁ + vol_w₂ × β₂ × spot_w₂

Where vol weights come from ∂σ_cross/∂σ_leg and spot weights from Cov(leg, cross)/Var(cross).

The Problem

This approach routes everything through USD. For GBPAUD with high GBPUSD/AUDUSD correlation (ρ = 0.66), the model attributed 99.9% of the cross beta to AUDUSD and only 0.1% to GBPUSD. GBP's contribution was nearly invisible because GBPUSD movements were almost entirely "explained" by AUDUSD through the correlation.

Result: the USD model predicted GBPAUD RR = +0.52%, but the market was +0.19%. The model couldn't see GBP-specific skew dynamics.

Limitation: The USD-only approach assumes constant correlation and decomposes everything through USD. It misses currency-specific skew dynamics that don't show up in the USD pair alone.

8. The Basket Approach — Currency-Level Betas

Instead of routing through USD, we assign each currency its own beta — estimated from ALL pairs involving that currency simultaneously.

Step 1: Basket Volatility

Each currency's "basket vol" measures how volatile it is across all its pairs:

BasketVol(X) = √(mean of σ²(X,j) for all j paired with X)
Example EUR appears in EURUSD (7.53%), EURJPY (10.80%), EURGBP (5.20%), EURCNH (6.38%)
BasketVol(EUR) = √((7.53² + 10.80² + 5.20² + 6.38²) / 4) = 7.76%

Step 2: Pair Weight Decomposition

For each pair, decompose its variance into contributions from each currency:

w_LHS = BasketVar(LHS) / (BasketVar(LHS) + BasketVar(RHS))
w_RHS = BasketVar(RHS) / (BasketVar(LHS) + BasketVar(RHS))
GBPAUD weights BasketVol(GBP) = 8.12%, BasketVol(AUD) = 9.40%
w_GBP = 8.12² / (8.12² + 9.40²) = 65.9 / 154.3 = 0.43
w_AUD = 9.40² / (8.12² + 9.40²) = 88.4 / 154.3 = 0.57

Compare to the USD-only model where GBP had only 0.1% weight — the basket approach gives GBP 43%.

Step 3: The Model

Each pair's beta is a weighted combination of its two currencies' betas:

β(pair_AB) = w_A × β_A − w_B × β_B

When pair AB goes up (A strengthens, B weakens), the vol change is w_A times A's beta (A strengthening) minus w_B times B's beta (B weakening).

Step 4: Solve by Least Squares

Each observed pair beta gives one equation. With N currencies and M pairs (M >> N), we have an overdetermined system. Least squares finds the currency betas that minimise the total squared error across all pairs.

We add one constraint: Σ β_currencies = 0, which pins the absolute level (just like FX rates — only differences are observable).

System of equations (simplified 3-currency example) EURUSD: 0.56 × β_EUR − 0.44 × β_USD = −0.71
USDJPY: 0.41 × β_USD − 0.59 × β_JPY = −0.27
EURJPY: 0.47 × β_EUR − 0.53 × β_JPY = −0.22
Constraint: β_EUR + β_USD + β_JPY = 0

Solution: β_EUR = −0.64, β_USD = +0.33, β_JPY = +0.31

9. Summary

The complete chain:

1 The RR prices spot-vol correlation (beta) through the vanna P&L mechanism

2 For 25d options, the formula simplifies to: β = 1.48 × RR / (ATM × √T)

3 This was verified by Monte Carlo: delta-hedging an RR at the model price produces zero P&L

4 Cross-pair RR can be predicted from currency-level betas using basket vol weights

5 Least squares extracts each currency's universal beta from the full cross-section of pair RRs

6 Residuals flag pair-specific mispricings — potential trade signals
