Vol-of-Vol (ν_log)

Extracting implied vol-of-vol from the 25-delta butterfly, mirroring the RR / β framework.

1. The Butterfly — What Is It Pricing?

The 25-delta butterfly (BF) is the vol premium of the wings over ATM:

BF = ½ × (σ_25d_call + σ_25d_put) − σ_ATM

BF is always positive — the wings trade at higher vol than ATM. Why? Because options at 25-delta have exposure to vol-of-vol: if vol moves around during the option's life, the wings benefit more than ATM.

If the RR is a vanna instrument (pricing spot-vol correlation), the BF is a volga instrument (pricing vol-of-vol).

Core insight: The BF prices how much vol moves around — the volatility of volatility. Just as RR → β (spot-vol beta), BF → ν_log (vol-of-vol).

2. Volga — The Greek That Drives the BF

Volga measures how an option's vega changes when vol moves:

Volga = ∂Vega / ∂σ = Vega × d₁ × d₂ / σ

For ATM options: d₁ ≈ 0, so d₁ × d₂ ≈ 0 — ATM has near-zero volga. Vol moving doesn't change ATM's vega much.

For 25-delta options: |d₁| ≈ 0.67, |d₂| ≈ 0.67–0.70 — both are significantly non-zero, so the wings have substantial positive volga. When vol rises, the wings pick up more vega (they move closer to ATM). When vol falls, they lose vega.

The BF structure (long wings, short ATM) therefore has large positive volga. If vol moves around, the BF holder earns volga P&L:

Volga P&L per step = ½ × Volga_BF × (Δσ)²

The ½ is the Taylor coefficient. Unlike vanna P&L (which depends on direction — does vol go up when spot goes up?), volga P&L depends only on the magnitude of vol moves. Vol up: earn. Vol down: earn. Either way, the wings benefit.

Volga of the 25d strangle

Volga_call = Vega × d₁_call × d₂_call / σ
Volga_put  = Vega × d₁_put × d₂_put / σ

Volga_strangle = Vega × (d₁_c × d₂_c + d₁_p × d₂_p) / σ

For 25-delta: d₁_c × d₂_c ≈ 0.470, d₁_p × d₂_p ≈ 0.440. Sum ≈ 0.910.

Worked Example: EURUSD 1M, σ = 7.70% d₁_call = −0.6745, d₂_call = −0.6966 → d₁×d₂ = 0.470
d₁_put = +0.6745, d₂_put = +0.6524 → d₁×d₂ = 0.440

Volga_strangle = Vega × 0.910 / 0.077 = Vega × 11.8

For 100 mio EUR: Vega = $104,000/volpt
Volga = $104,000 × 11.8 = $1,228,000 per (vol pt)²

Meaning: if vol moves by 1 vol pt (7.70% → 8.70%), the strangle's vega increases by $1.23M per vol pt. It picks up more vol exposure as vol rises.

3. Vol-of-Vol — The ν_log Parameter

We model vol dynamics as lognormal:

dσ / σ = ν_log × dW

This means vol moves proportionally to its level — if ATM is 10%, vol moves are larger in absolute terms than if ATM is 5%. This matches the observed behaviour that high-vol regimes have larger absolute vol swings.

Under this model, the daily 1-sigma vol change (in vol points) is:

Daily σ stdev = σ_ATM × ν_log × √(1/252) ≈ σ_ATM × ν_log / 16
Example ATM = 7.70%, ν_log = 1.95
Daily σ move ≈ 7.70 × 1.95 / 16 ≈ 0.94 vol pts

On a typical day, 1M EURUSD vol might move ±0.94 vol pts (1σ).
That's 7.70% → 8.64% or 7.70% → 6.76%.

4. From ν_log to Fair BF — The Volga P&L Argument

The logic mirrors the RR derivation exactly:

1 The BF holder earns volga P&L when vol moves: ½ × Volga × (Δσ)²

2 Under lognormal dynamics: (Δσ)² = σ² × ν² × dt per step

3 Over the option life, sum with a scaling factor SF ≈ 0.337 (from MC — volga decays as vol drifts from inception, similar to the 0.5 factor for vanna but smaller because volga decays faster):

E[Volga P&L] = SF × ½ × Volga_strangle × σ² × ν² × T

4 Substitute Volga_strangle = Vega × 0.910 / σ:

E[Volga P&L] = 0.337 × 0.5 × Vega × 0.910 / σ × σ² × ν² × T
             = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × Vega × σ × ν² × T

5 Set this equal to the BF premium. The BF costs approximately 2 × Vega × BF (each wing at BF extra vol):

2 × Vega × BF = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × Vega × σ × ν² × T

BF = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × 0.5 × σ × ν² × T

BF = 0.075 × σ × ν² × T

The constant 0.075 packs four factors:

FactorValueOrigin
Taylor coefficient0.5½ × Volga × (Δσ)²
Volga scaling (SF)0.337MC: expected volga over life ≈ 34% of inception
d₁×d₂ sum (25d)0.910= 0.470 + 0.440 for call + put
Premium normalization0.5Premium = 2 × Vega × BF

Inverting to extract ν_log from market BF:

ν_log = √( BF / (0.075 × ATM × T) )

Where BF and ATM are in the same units (both % or both decimal), T in years.

Worked Example: EURUSD 1M ATM = 7.70%, BF = 0.18%, T = 30/365 = 0.0822

ν_log = √(0.18 / (0.075 × 7.70 × 0.0822))
     = √(0.18 / 0.0475)
     = √3.79
     = 1.95

Daily vol move ≈ 7.70 × 1.95 / 16 ≈ 0.94 vol pts per day

5. The Market Maker Proof

We verified the formula via Monte Carlo, mirroring the RR verification:

1 Price the vega-neutral 25d BF structure: long strangle at wing vols, short 0.797 × ATM straddle

2 Simulate 200k paths: spot moves at implied vol, vol follows dσ/σ = ν × dW (independent of spot, ρ = 0)

3 Delta hedge daily, mark to market

4 Measure: does the volga P&L equal the BF premium?

Result: Recovery ratio = 0.90. The delta-hedged BF structure earns 90% of the premium back through volga P&L. The ~10% shortfall is the known lognormal-dispersion bias — under lognormal dynamics, vol can move very far from inception, making the quadratic volga approximation increasingly inaccurate for the paths that contribute most to the P&L.

Important subtlety: the MC must test the full vega-neutral BF (strangle minus k × straddle), not the naked strangle. The strangle alone gives ~2.0× recovery because it has positive vega exposure that generates additional P&L beyond the volga channel. The straddle hedge neutralizes this, isolating pure volga P&L.

6. Why Lognormal?

We could model vol as normal (dσ = ν_norm × dW) instead of lognormal (dσ/σ = ν_log × dW). Both give the same daily vol move today:

Normal: daily Δσ = ν_norm / √252
Lognormal: daily Δσ = σ × ν_log / √252

Set equal today: ν_norm = σ_today × ν_log

But they diverge when vol changes:

NormalLognormal
σ at 5%Same daily movesSmaller daily moves
σ at 15%Same daily movesLarger daily moves
σ can go negative?Yes (problem)No (stays positive)

Lognormal matches the observed behaviour: high-vol regimes have larger absolute vol swings. It also prevents vol from going negative. This is why we use lognormal.

7. What ν_log Absorbs

The BF-implied ν_log embeds more than just diffusive vol-of-vol. It also absorbs:

Jump risk premium. The BF prices tail protection — if vol can jump (e.g., on an event), the wings become more valuable. Since our model has no jumps (only diffusion), the jump premium shows up as a higher ν_log. Short-tenor BF tends to have elevated ν_log partly because of this.

Smile convexity beyond volga. The Taylor approximation (½ × Volga × Δσ²) captures the second-order effect. Higher-order terms (how volga itself changes when vol moves) contribute to the BF but aren't in our formula — they inflate the implied ν_log slightly.

This means the implied ν_log is an upper bound on the true diffusive vol-of-vol. The gap between implied and realized ν_log includes the jump premium and model approximation error.

What it does not absorb: skew. One might worry that big-RR pairs have inflated BFs (skew curvature feeding the wings), contaminating ν_log with β². MC says no — the fly prices vol variance orthogonal to spot, and the β² contribution is tiny and slightly negative (Section 10). BF-implied ν_log reads the idiosyncratic vol-of-vol cleanly.

9. Currency-Level Decomposition — One Global Vol Factor + Idiosyncratic

Just as we decompose RR into currency betas, we decompose BF into currency-level vol-of-vols. But vol-of-vol has a structural feature skew does not: it doesn't diversify in crosses. When vol-of-vol hits, it hits everything — EURUSD, USDJPY and EURJPY vols all start swinging together. The data says so too: observed cross ν's sit at the same level as USD-pair ν's, where independent currency vol-of-vols would predict crosses ~30% lower.

So each currency's vol shock carries one global vol factor, shared by all currencies, plus an idiosyncratic part:

d ln σ_i = ν_g × dW_global + η_i × dW_i

Aggregating to a pair with the basket variance weights (w_A + w_B = 1):

ν²_AB = ν_g² + w_A² × η_A² + w_B² × η_B²

The global factor passes through undiversified — its weights sum to one — while the idiosyncratic parts combine in quadrature (w² weights) and partially cancel. That one feature reproduces the empirical fact the old ρ_vol = 0 model could not: crosses are not calmer than their legs in vol-of-vol terms.

A correction along the way

An earlier version of this page argued for plain-w "weighted average" weights instead of w², because w² "systematically underpredicted every pair's BF". That was a symptom patch: with weights summing to one, the linear-w formula behaves like perfectly correlated currency vol-of-vols (ρ_vol ≈ 1) — it kept the level right for the wrong reason. The global factor is the honest version of the same fact: it makes the common component explicit and lets the idiosyncratic parts combine correctly in quadrature.

How ν_g is actually backed out — the exact conditions

We never "solve" for ν_g directly — there isn't a unique algebraic answer, and understanding why is the whole game. Each pair gives one equation, ν²_AB = ν_g² + w_A²η_A² + w_B²η_B². But if you raise ν_g² by a small amount and lower every η² to compensate, the predictions barely move (because the w² in front of each η² sum to roughly ½ for most pairs). So a whole family of (ν_g, η) combinations fits almost equally well — the fit error is flat along a ridge. This is the same illness as the undetermined level of the betas, which we cured with Σβ = 0.

So we impose four conditions and let them pin a single answer:

1. One common factorevery pair shares exactly one global vol shock ν_g (rank-1 common part)
2. ν_g ≥ 0a volatility, so non-negative
3. η_i² ≥ 0each idiosyncratic variance is non-negative — enforced by non-negative least squares (NNLS). This is what stops the compensation from running forever: push ν_g too high and some η² wants to go negative, gets floored at 0, and the fit finally breaks.
4. Max-ν_g anchoramong all (ν_g, η) within 2% of the best fit (the flat ridge), take the largest ν_g

Mechanically: sweep ν_g from 0 upward; at each value, NNLS finds the best η² ≥ 0; record the fit error. The error stays flat across a plateau, then degrades once condition 3 starts binding. Condition 4 says walk to the far (high-ν_g) end of that plateau and stop.

What condition 4 means economically: "attribute as much vol-of-vol as the data allows to the shared factor, and only call something idiosyncratic if the fit forces you to." It's a modeling choice, not a law — the honest analog of Σβ = 0. A different anchor (say, fixing the most-global currency's η to zero) gives almost the same answer. The number ν_g ≈ 1.79 is "the largest common vol-of-vol consistent with the butterfly cross-section," not a uniquely determined constant.
Results (1M, 8 currencies, 16 pairs) ν_g ≈ 1.79 — the global factor carries ~80% of average pair vol-of-vol variance.
Idio η ranking: JPY 1.80, NZD 1.39, USD 1.27, AUD 1.11, CHF 0.89 — EUR, GBP, CNH ≈ pure global factor.
RMSE of BF residuals: 0.025%.

The tool shows ν_g and the global share above the currency table, with η (idio) and ν total = √(ν_g² + η²) per currency.
Reading the residuals: AUDNZD comes out over-predicted while the risk crosses (AUDJPY, NZDJPY, NZDUSD) under-predict. That's the stochastic-correlation channel: tight bloc pairs feel the global vol factor less, because their spot correlation rises exactly when global vol does. A per-pair global loading tied to spot correlation is the natural next extension.

10. Does Skew Feed the Fly? (β² and the Butterfly)

Desk intuition says a long fly is "long RR gamma": vanna-flat at inception, but as spot rallies the position picks up vanna — it gets longer RR. If vol genuinely responds to spot (β ≠ 0), that developing vanna pays. Shouldn't the fly then be worth more in a high-β pair?

In our units the question is sharp, because β is a vol-of-vol. β is vol points per 1% spot move, and spot's annualized 1σ move in percent is σ itself — so the spot-driven part of dσ/σ has annualized stdev of exactly β:

dσ / σ = β × dW_spot + ν_idio × dW_idio   ⇒   ν_total² = β² + ν_idio²

If the fly priced total vol variance, fair BF would be 0.075 × ATM × T × (β² + ν_idio²). The MC says it does not:

MC verdict (bf_beta_convexity.py) Delta-hedged vega-neutral fly (25d strangle − vega-ratio × straddle) under dσ/σ = β·dW_spot + ν·dW_idio.
ν_idio² enters the fly with coefficient ≈ +0.90 (the validated baseline).
β² enters with coefficient ≈ −0.11 — small and negative, not +1.

At a hefty β = 1.0 the BF correction is ~0.006 vol pts — under a basis point.

The economics: the fly prices vol variance orthogonal to spot. Spot-driven vol moves are spanned by spot — hedgeable, and already charged for in the RR through vanna. Conditional on the spot path, β-driven vol carries no remaining uncertainty, so it commands no convexity premium. The slight negative is the SABR (2 − 3ρ²) effect: at |ρ| = 1, the vol-of-vol budget is spent on skew — with interest.

Division of labour: RR prices the spot-spanned vol risk (β); BF prices the spot-orthogonal vol risk (ν_idio). The "long RR gamma" carry on a long fly is real — but it is fully paid for in fair value. Practical upshot: BF-implied ν_log is clean of skew at realistic β, and the decomposition in Section 9 operates on genuinely orthogonal quantities.

11. Spot Correlation vs Vol Correlation — Three Different Things

The factor model quietly hands us something new: an implied correlation between currencies' vols, ρ_vol(L,R) = ν_g² / (ν_L·ν_R). Two currencies' vols are correlated only through the shock they share — the global factor — so the formula is just "how much of each one's vol-of-vol is the common part." It's an output of the butterfly fit, not an input. But it's easy to confuse with spot correlation, so let's be precise.

First: what "orthogonal" means

Two shocks are orthogonal when they're uncorrelated — when one fires it tells you nothing about the other. The word is geometric: think of shocks as arrows. Same direction → they reinforce; opposite → they cancel; perpendicular → independent, and their combined size obeys Pythagoras, a² + b² = c². That is exactly why our variances add with no cross term:

ν² = ν_g² + η²   (no 2·cov term — because global ⊥ idiosyncratic)

If the global factor and a currency's own vol shock were not orthogonal, that clean sum would gain a term + 2·ρ·ν_g·η. We assumed it away. (Concretely: a BoJ surprise to JPY vol is assumed equally likely whether or not global vol is spiking. For JPY that assumption is the most suspect one in the whole model.)

Three correlations that get confused

Correlation of…What it measuresWhere it comes from
spot returns (ρ)do the two pairs move up/down together (direction)the basket / cross triangle
squared returns (= ρ²)do the big-move days line up (size, ignoring direction)a math identity about returns
vol-of-vol (ν_g²/ν_Lν_R)do the two vol levels drift up/down together over weeksfit from butterfly prices

Under joint normality, squaring spot correlation gives the correlation of squared returns: Corr(r_L², r_R²) = ρ². That is true — but it is the middle row: whether two pairs have their big days on the same days. It is not the bottom row. The vol-of-vol matrix measures the slow drift of the vol level between calm and nervous regimes, which is dominated by the common risk factor and is fit independently from option prices. So the butterfly-implied vol correlation does not equal ρ², and does not come from spot correlation at all. They would coincide only in a make-believe world where vol is nothing but trailing realized variance with no regime of its own.

The one real bridge — the betas. Spot correlation does leak into vol co-movement, but through one specific channel: the leverage term β_L·β_R·ρ_spot (first power of ρ, scaled by both betas). That is the difference between the two correlation matrices in the tool — "BF only" (ν_g²/ν_Lν_R) versus "leverage-adjusted" (adds β_L·β_R·ρ_spot up top). The basket gives spot correlation; the butterflies give vol correlation; the betas are the bridge.

Why one factor isn't enough — blocs

With a single global factor, a currency's only way to correlate with others is its loading on that one shared shock. So a "mostly global" currency (η ≈ 0) is forced to look ρ ≈ 1 to everyone who is also mostly global — you can see it in the BF-only matrix, where EUR, GBP and CNH are 1.00 to each other and equally high to the rest. The model literally cannot express "EUR vol is especially tied to CHF, more than to JPY."

But that EUR–CHF bloc is economically real (ECB/SNB, EURCHF management) and has nothing to do with JPY. To represent it you add a second factor — a regional "European vol" shock that EUR and CHF both load on but JPY doesn't. One factor = a single global weather system everyone shares; two factors = global weather plus regional weather, so currencies in the same region co-move more than either does with an outsider. Standard factor-model logic (one market factor + sector factors).

Next frontier — vol of correlation. We now have σ (ATM), β (RR), ν (BF) and ρ_spot (basket). The last missing piece is the volatility of implied correlation itself. It falls right out of this toolkit: a cross's correlation is a fixed function of three vols (ρ = (σ_X²+σ_Y²−σ_C²)/(2σ_Xσ_Y)), so its movement is driven by those three vols' vol-of-vols and how they co-move — i.e. ν and the very vol-of-vol correlation matrix above. The leftover stochastic-correlation signal is exactly the AUDNZD-type residual in Section 9.
