Vol-of-Vol (ν_log)

Extracting implied vol-of-vol from the 25-delta butterfly, mirroring the RR / β framework.

1. The Butterfly — What Is It Pricing?

The 25-delta butterfly (BF) is the vol premium of the wings over ATM:

BF = ½ × (σ_25d_call + σ_25d_put) − σ_ATM

BF is always positive — the wings trade at higher vol than ATM. Why? Because options at 25-delta have exposure to vol-of-vol: if vol moves around during the option's life, the wings benefit more than ATM.

If the RR is a vanna instrument (pricing spot-vol correlation), the BF is a volga instrument (pricing vol-of-vol).

Core insight: The BF prices how much vol moves around — the volatility of volatility. Just as RR → β (spot-vol beta), BF → ν_log (vol-of-vol).

2. Volga — The Greek That Drives the BF

Volga measures how an option's vega changes when vol moves:

Volga = ∂Vega / ∂σ = Vega × d₁ × d₂ / σ

For ATM options: d₁ ≈ 0, so d₁ × d₂ ≈ 0 — ATM has near-zero volga. Vol moving doesn't change ATM's vega much.

For 25-delta options: |d₁| ≈ 0.67, |d₂| ≈ 0.67–0.70 — both are significantly non-zero, so the wings have substantial positive volga. When vol rises, the wings pick up more vega (they move closer to ATM). When vol falls, they lose vega.

The BF structure (long wings, short ATM) therefore has large positive volga. If vol moves around, the BF holder earns volga P&L:

Volga P&L per step = ½ × Volga_BF × (Δσ)²

The ½ is the Taylor coefficient. Unlike vanna P&L (which depends on direction — does vol go up when spot goes up?), volga P&L depends only on the magnitude of vol moves. Vol up: earn. Vol down: earn. Either way, the wings benefit.

Volga of the 25d strangle

Volga_call = Vega × d₁_call × d₂_call / σ
Volga_put  = Vega × d₁_put × d₂_put / σ

Volga_strangle = Vega × (d₁_c × d₂_c + d₁_p × d₂_p) / σ

For 25-delta: d₁_c × d₂_c ≈ 0.470, d₁_p × d₂_p ≈ 0.440. Sum ≈ 0.910.

Worked Example: EURUSD 1M, σ = 7.70% d₁_call = −0.6745, d₂_call = −0.6966 → d₁×d₂ = 0.470
d₁_put = +0.6745, d₂_put = +0.6524 → d₁×d₂ = 0.440

Volga_strangle = Vega × 0.910 / 0.077 = Vega × 11.8

For 100 mio EUR: Vega = $104,000/volpt
Volga = $104,000 × 11.8 = $1,228,000 per (vol pt)²

Meaning: if vol moves by 1 vol pt (7.70% → 8.70%), the strangle's vega increases by $1.23M per vol pt. It picks up more vol exposure as vol rises.

3. Vol-of-Vol — The ν_log Parameter

We model vol dynamics as lognormal:

dσ / σ = ν_log × dW

This means vol moves proportionally to its level — if ATM is 10%, vol moves are larger in absolute terms than if ATM is 5%. This matches the observed behaviour that high-vol regimes have larger absolute vol swings.

Under this model, the daily 1-sigma vol change (in vol points) is:

Daily σ stdev = σ_ATM × ν_log × √(1/252) ≈ σ_ATM × ν_log / 16
Example ATM = 7.70%, ν_log = 1.95
Daily σ move ≈ 7.70 × 1.95 / 16 ≈ 0.94 vol pts

On a typical day, 1M EURUSD vol might move ±0.94 vol pts (1σ).
That's 7.70% → 8.64% or 7.70% → 6.76%.

4. From ν_log to Fair BF — The Volga P&L Argument

The logic mirrors the RR derivation exactly:

1 The BF holder earns volga P&L when vol moves: ½ × Volga × (Δσ)²

2 Under lognormal dynamics: (Δσ)² = σ² × ν² × dt per step

3 Over the option life, sum with a scaling factor SF ≈ 0.337 (from MC — volga decays as vol drifts from inception, similar to the 0.5 factor for vanna but smaller because volga decays faster):

E[Volga P&L] = SF × ½ × Volga_strangle × σ² × ν² × T

4 Substitute Volga_strangle = Vega × 0.910 / σ:

E[Volga P&L] = 0.337 × 0.5 × Vega × 0.910 / σ × σ² × ν² × T
             = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × Vega × σ × ν² × T

5 Set this equal to the BF premium. The BF costs approximately 2 × Vega × BF (each wing at BF extra vol):

2 × Vega × BF = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × Vega × σ × ν² × T

BF = 0.337 × 0.5 × 0.910 × 0.5 × σ × ν² × T

BF = 0.075 × σ × ν² × T

The constant 0.075 packs four factors:

FactorValueOrigin
Taylor coefficient0.5½ × Volga × (Δσ)²
Volga scaling (SF)0.337MC: expected volga over life ≈ 34% of inception
d₁×d₂ sum (25d)0.910= 0.470 + 0.440 for call + put
Premium normalization0.5Premium = 2 × Vega × BF

Inverting to extract ν_log from market BF:

ν_log = √( BF / (0.075 × ATM × T) )

Where BF and ATM are in the same units (both % or both decimal), T in years.

Worked Example: EURUSD 1M ATM = 7.70%, BF = 0.18%, T = 30/365 = 0.0822

ν_log = √(0.18 / (0.075 × 7.70 × 0.0822))
     = √(0.18 / 0.0475)
     = √3.79
     = 1.95

Daily vol move ≈ 7.70 × 1.95 / 16 ≈ 0.94 vol pts per day

5. The Market Maker Proof

We verified the formula via Monte Carlo, mirroring the RR verification:

1 Price the vega-neutral 25d BF structure: long strangle at wing vols, short 0.797 × ATM straddle

2 Simulate 200k paths: spot moves at implied vol, vol follows dσ/σ = ν × dW (independent of spot, ρ = 0)

3 Delta hedge daily, mark to market

4 Measure: does the volga P&L equal the BF premium?

Result: Recovery ratio = 0.90. The delta-hedged BF structure earns 90% of the premium back through volga P&L. The ~10% shortfall is the known lognormal-dispersion bias — under lognormal dynamics, vol can move very far from inception, making the quadratic volga approximation increasingly inaccurate for the paths that contribute most to the P&L.

Important subtlety: the MC must test the full vega-neutral BF (strangle minus k × straddle), not the naked strangle. The strangle alone gives ~2.0× recovery because it has positive vega exposure that generates additional P&L beyond the volga channel. The straddle hedge neutralizes this, isolating pure volga P&L.

6. Why Lognormal?

We could model vol as normal (dσ = ν_norm × dW) instead of lognormal (dσ/σ = ν_log × dW). Both give the same daily vol move today:

Normal: daily Δσ = ν_norm / √252
Lognormal: daily Δσ = σ × ν_log / √252

Set equal today: ν_norm = σ_today × ν_log

But they diverge when vol changes:

NormalLognormal
σ at 5%Same daily movesSmaller daily moves
σ at 15%Same daily movesLarger daily moves
σ can go negative?Yes (problem)No (stays positive)

Lognormal matches the observed behaviour: high-vol regimes have larger absolute vol swings. It also prevents vol from going negative. This is why we use lognormal.

7. What ν_log Absorbs

The BF-implied ν_log embeds more than just diffusive vol-of-vol. It also absorbs:

Jump risk premium. The BF prices tail protection — if vol can jump (e.g., on an event), the wings become more valuable. Since our model has no jumps (only diffusion), the jump premium shows up as a higher ν_log. Short-tenor BF tends to have elevated ν_log partly because of this.

Smile convexity beyond volga. The Taylor approximation (½ × Volga × Δσ²) captures the second-order effect. Higher-order terms (how volga itself changes when vol moves) contribute to the BF but aren't in our formula — they inflate the implied ν_log slightly.

This means the implied ν_log is an upper bound on the true diffusive vol-of-vol. The gap between implied and realized ν_log includes the jump premium and model approximation error.

9. Currency-Level ν_log Decomposition

Just as we decompose RR into currency betas, we decompose BF into currency-level vol-of-vols. For each pair AB:

ν²_AB = w_A × ν_A² + w_B × ν_B²

This assumes ρ_vol = 0 (independent currency vol-of-vols) as a baseline. The weights w_A, w_B are the same basket vol weights used for β — not squared.

Why weighted average, not portfolio variance?

An earlier version used w² weights (the portfolio variance formula: Var(wX) = w²Var(X)). This systematically underpredicted every pair's BF because w_A² + w_B² < 1 when w_A + w_B = 1. For example, a 50/50 pair would have predicted ν² = 0.25ν_A² + 0.25ν_B² — only half the average.

The correct formula is a weighted average of ν² values. The pair's vol-of-vol² is a blend of each currency's vol-of-vol², weighted by how much each currency drives the pair. If a pair is 70% driven by AUD and 30% by GBP, its ν² should be 70% of AUD's ν² + 30% of GBP's ν² — not 49% + 9%.

Comparison with β: Both use the same generic basket weights w_A, w_B. β combines them linearly (β = w_A × β_A − w_B × β_B) because skew is directional. ν² combines them as a weighted average (ν² = w_A × ν_A² + w_B × ν_B²) because vol-of-vol is a magnitude — each currency contributes proportionally to the pair's vol uncertainty.

Solve for currency ν² values via non-negative least squares (ν must be positive, unlike β which can be negative). The residuals flag pairs with excess or deficient BF relative to what the currency vol-of-vols predict.

Results (1M, 8 currencies, 16 pairs) RMSE of BF residuals: 0.022% — much tighter than β residuals (0.24%).

This suggests the BF surface is more internally consistent than the RR surface.
The "cross-pair BF mispricing" signal is smaller than the RR signal.

3 Daily vol move ≈ ATM × ν_log / 16 (practical grip)

4 Verified by MC: vega-neutral BF delta-hedged → 90% premium recovery

5 Lognormal model: vol moves scale with vol level, can't go negative
